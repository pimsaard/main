Mapping the Mind: How to Apply the Repertory Grid Technique in Applied Linguistics

By Assoc. Prof. Pariwat Imsa-ard, Ph.D.

As applied linguistics turns increasingly towards subjectivity and individualized meaning-making, we face a methodological dilemma: How do we systematically measure tacit beliefs without flattening the very individuality we want to explore?

Large-scale surveys prioritize aggregate trends over personal meaning, and predefined thematic coding schemes often narrow the range of participant expression. The tacit belief systems that quietly steer classroom interactions, assessment choices, and learning trajectories remain methodologically elusive.

Enter the Repertory Grid Technique (RGT). Grounded in George Kelly’s (1955) Personal Construct Theory, RGT is a flexible and rigorous tool that serves as a conceptual lens through which subjectivity can be explored systematically. It bridges the gap between rich qualitative narratives and analyzable, quantitative data matrices.

How RGT Works: A Step-by-Step Guide

At its core, an RGT study is a structured conversation. Instead of testing hypotheses using researcher-supplied categories, RGT allows participants to articulate their own evaluative continua.

Here is a breakdown of the standard workflow to apply RGT to your own data collection:

Step 1

Element Creation

The researcher defines the topic of inquiry by creating a list of concrete and comparable items—known as "elements"—that are familiar and relevant to the participant. Good practice recommends a set of 6 to 12 elements. This range balances analytic richness with participant fatigue.

Example Scenario

To study teachers' beliefs about error correction, your elements could be specific actions: 'Recasting a student's mistake immediately', 'Using a specific hand gesture', 'Asking another student for the correct answer', 'Ignoring slips of the tongue', and 'Providing written feedback on a draft'.

Step 2

Construct Elicitation

This is where the magic happens. The researcher presents the elements in groups of three (a triadic comparison) and asks a simple but powerful question: "In what way are two of these alike, yet different from the third?"

The resulting answer yields a bipolar distinction, known as a personal construct. Because every construct is elicited in the respondent’s own vocabulary, the risk of imposing researcher categories is sharply reduced.

Example Scenario

A teacher is shown three elements: 'Recasting a student's mistake', 'Using a hand gesture', and 'Providing written feedback'. The teacher might say, "Recasting and gestures are done in the moment, while written feedback is delayed and more planned." You have now elicited the bipolar construct: "In-the-moment vs. Delayed and planned".

Step 3

Gridding and Rating

Once a satisfactory pool of constructs has been elicited (typically 6-15), the participant completes a matrix. They rate every single element against each elicited construct, typically using a 5-point or 7-point scale anchored by their own words.

Example Scenario

Using the construct 'In-the-moment (1) vs. Delayed and planned (5)', the teacher rates all elements. They would likely rate 'Recasting a student's mistake' as a 1, while rating 'Providing written feedback on a draft' as a 5. The resulting matrix is simultaneously idiographic (preserving their language) and nomothetic (permitting statistical interrogation).

Step 4

Post-Grid Reflection

The repertory grid should not be treated as an endpoint. In this final phase, the researcher and participant discuss the completed grid to uncover deeper qualitative insights, explore surprising rating patterns, and identify which constructs are most central to their perspective.

Example Scenario

The researcher asks, "You rated 'Ignoring slips of the tongue' as a 3, right in the middle. Why is that?" The teacher might explain, "Because ignoring it is an instant decision, but I make a mental note to address it later, so it's a bit of both." This meta-commentary deeply enriches data credibility.

Why RGT Matters for the Future of Research

Applying the Repertory Grid Technique does not replace familiar mixed-methods designs, but rather renegotiates their epistemic balance. It gives primacy to participant-generated categories without abandoning systematic comparison.

When executed with ethical sensitivity and methodological rigor, RGT enriches both the descriptive and explanatory power of applied-linguistics inquiry, paving the way for research that is simultaneously human-centered and theoretically impactful.
